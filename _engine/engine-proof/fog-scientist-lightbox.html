Occam's Razor

Read the films. Strip the assumptions. Find the simplest sufficient explanation — and learn where the cut goes too far.

Film 01 The Backlit Panel

You are a radiologist at a backlit panel.

A case comes in as a tangle. Five explanations clipped over one image, each plausible, each casting its own shadow.

The amateur adds films. The expert removes them. Occam's razor is not a rule about truth — it is a rule about where to look first. When competing explanations fit the same evidence, start with the one carrying the fewest assumptions.

Film 03 What You Just Felt

Simplest sufficient — not simplest possible.

If you peeled the load-bearing film, the image broke. That is the lesson the meter cannot teach in words: there is a floor. Cut past the explanation that still accounts for all the evidence, and you have not found elegance — you have found error.

The razor trims assumptions. It does not trim facts.

A cough plus a fever plus a chest film with a clear infiltrate needs one sufficient cause. The cats, the flight, the cigarettes — extra entities the evidence does not demand. But the infiltrate itself is data. Remove your acknowledgment of it and the simplest story stops being true.

Film 04 The Two Cuts

Two ways to use a blade.

✓ Cut here

Rival theories that each add an unobserved cause. The exotic infection. The rare syndrome. The conspiracy of coincidences. None earns its keep while a plainer cause fits.

✕ Keep this

Any explanation the evidence actively requires. If the data only fits a more complex model, the complexity is real. The razor never overrides observation.

Calibration is the whole craft: knowing the difference between an assumption you imported and a fact the world handed you.

Film 05 At the Table With a Client

The tangle a person brings.

A client arrives with a knot: the launch failed, the team is misaligned, the market shifted, the tool broke, morale is low, and possibly the strategy was wrong from the start. Six films, one outcome.

  • Resist the grand theory that requires all six to be true at once.
  • Ask: what is the smallest set that fully explains the failure?
  • Usually one or two films account for everything. The rest are shadows.
  • But verify the floor — if the evidence needs the complex story, keep it.
Film 06 The Failure Mode

When the razor lies.

Heuristics are confident. That is their danger.

Occam's razor feels like rigor, so it gets reached for when the simple answer is merely the comfortable one. Fraud is simpler than a complex accounting error — until it isn't. A single cause feels cleaner than three interacting ones — until the system is genuinely coupled.

The fix is not to abandon the razor. It is to hold it as a starting hypothesis, not a verdict. Look at the simplest first. Then test whether it actually suffices.

Film 07 The One Move

The practice, in a sentence.

When explanations compete, sort them by assumptions, test the cheapest first — and stop cutting the moment one still explains everything.

That is the entire discipline. Not "the simple answer is right." Rather: look where the assumptions are fewest, then earn your way back up only as far as the evidence forces you.

Film 08 · Off The Record

Occam never owned the razor.

William of Ockham, a 14th-century English friar, never wrote the famous line "entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity." That phrasing was coined three centuries later by another scholar, John Punch. Ockham did argue the principle — but the elegant sentence everyone quotes is itself an invented entity, attached to him for no reason the evidence requires. The razor, by its own logic, should have trimmed his name off it. It is perhaps the only principle in philosophy that quietly violates itself in the citation.
