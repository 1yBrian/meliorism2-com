Occam’s Razor

Strip a problem down to the fewest parts that still explain it. Then know when not to.

A workbench, not a lecture  ·  build  test  refine
Part 01On the bench

Pull up a stool. We’re building a model.

I have sat across from the tangle you’re holding. A client describes a problem and ten possible causes spill out at once. The temptation is to honor all ten.

Don’t. On this bench we build explanations the way a model-maker builds: start with raw parts, clamp the thing in the vise, add only what the evidence forces you to add. Every assumption is a part you bolt on. Parts cost weight. Weight costs truth.

// the heuristic, plain: when explanations compete, reach first for the one carrying the fewest assumptions.

Part 02The blade

What the razor actually cuts

“Entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity.”

William of Ockham, a 14th-century friar, never said it in those exact words — but the instinct is his. The razor doesn’t say the simple answer is true. It says the simple answer is where you should look first, because every extra assumption is one more thing that has to independently be correct.

Three assumptions stacked, each 80% likely to hold, leave you at barely half. The blade trims the stack.

Part 04Too few parts

The bare frame falls over

Strip too far and you’re not being elegant — you’re being lazy. “The client is just unmotivated” is a one-part model. It fits nothing and predicts nothing. It’s a label wearing the costume of an explanation.

The razor removes unnecessary assumptions, not necessary ones. Ockham’s own phrase ends with beyond necessity. Keep every part the evidence demands. Cut only what it doesn’t.

Part 05Too many parts

The over-built model that only fits today

This is the failure mode I see most in skilled practitioners. You add a part for every wrinkle: the childhood, the boss, the Mercury retrograde, the gut biome. Each part makes today’s case fit a little tighter. The model becomes a beautiful, bespoke sculpture of this one person on this one Tuesday.

Then the next case walks in, and your masterpiece predicts nothing. You didn’t build a model. You memorized a moment. That’s overfitting — and the dial showed you its exact price: the teal line falling off the cliff.

Field note · spotting it live

If your explanation needs a new special reason for every new fact, you don’t have an explanation. You have an excuse generator. Real models get simpler as they get truer.

Part 06The edge case

When the complex answer is the real one

Here’s the part most people skip, and it’s the part that makes you a practitioner instead of a parrot. Simplest is not always true. Sometimes the tangle is the truth.

Rare diseases are rare for a reason — but they exist, and the patient in front of you might be the one. The razor tells you where to look first, not where to stop. The skill isn’t cutting. The skill is calibration: knowing when the simple model has stopped fitting the evidence, and being honest enough to add the part you were hoping not to need.

// rule of thumb: simple until the evidence breaks it. Then — and only then — add exactly one more part and test again.

Part 07At the bench tomorrow

Three moves you can run this week

  • 01
    Count the parts out loud. When you catch yourself explaining, literally count the assumptions. Three or more? Ask which one the evidence actually forces.
  • 02
    Run the next-case test. Before you commit to a theory, ask: would this still explain a different person with the same symptom? If not, you’ve overfit.
  • 03
    Name the part you’re avoiding. When the simple model keeps breaking, the complex answer is usually the one you didn’t want to be true. Say it. Test it.
Part 08Final pass

Lift it out of the vise

The model is built. It has the fewest parts that still explain the case — and it’ll hold up when the next one walks in. That’s the whole craft: not the simplest possible, but the simplest sufficient.

The better explanation was already there. We just stopped bolting parts onto it.

// you came in holding ten possible causes. You leave holding the two that were doing all the work.

Part 09Off the clock

A razor that shaved nothing

Ockham’s razor is named for a man who, as far as history records, never owned a razor for this idea, never drew a curve, and was excommunicated — not for the principle, but for arguing the Pope shouldn’t own property.

The crispest version we quote today — “entities must not be multiplied beyond necessity” — was written by John Punch, an Irish friar, three hundred years after Ockham died. We attached the sharpest line in philosophy to the wrong man and kept it there for centuries. The simplest story (one friar, one blade, one sentence) was, fittingly, too simple to be true.

— and that, off the record, is the most Occam thing about Occam’s Razor.
