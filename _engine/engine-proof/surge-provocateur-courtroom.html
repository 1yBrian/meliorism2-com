You are the judge. Four explanations want your belief. Most people convict the wrong one — because the elaborate story feels like the true one. Today you sharpen the blade that cuts assumptions away.

↓ Enter the courtroom
01 / Opening Statement

The blade, defined.

A 14th-century friar named William of Ockham left us one instruction sharp enough to survive 700 years: do not multiply entities beyond necessity.

Translated for people who don't speak medieval Latin: when two explanations both fit the facts, the one demanding fewer unproven assumptions is the better bet. Not the prettier one. Not the more dramatic one. The leaner one.

You don't reward the truest theory. You reward the one with the best story. Occam's razor is how you stop.

02 / The Evidence Board
Exhibit A — pinned to the wall

One event. Four suspects.

A real situation lands on your desk. Four competing explanations get pinned to the board, connected by red string. Each one could be true. Only one carries the lightest load of assumptions — and your instinct will fight you on which.

The Facts — undisputed

A six-person product team shipped its launch three weeks late. The lead, Maya, went quiet in the final fortnight — fewer messages, no standup updates, a missed demo. The work eventually arrived, polished but overdue. Now: why?

04 / Cross-Examination

Why the leanest survives.

Put each theory on the stand. The complex ones collapse not because they're impossible — but because every added assumption is another claim that has to also be true. Probability is multiplicative. Stack four maybes and you've built a house of maybes.

"Maya was secretly job-hunting and checked out."

Requires: a hidden job search, a decision to disengage, that the disengagement caused the delay, and that nobody noticed the search. Four unproven claims. Each plausible. Together, fragile.

"The original scope was bigger than two weeks of work."

Requires: one thing — that the estimate was wrong. One claim, and the most common failure mode in shipped software. It explains the silence (heads-down), the late demo (not ready), and the eventual polish (it got finished).

Notice what the lean theory does: it explains all the facts with the fewest moving parts. That's not luck. That's the razor working.

05 / The Dissent

When the blade lies.

Mistrial Risk

Simplest is not the same as true.

Occam's razor tells you where to look first — not what you'll find. The simplest sufficient explanation is the best opening hypothesis, not a verdict. Sometimes the elaborate story is the real one: conspiracies happen, rare diseases exist, the quiet teammate really was burning out.

The razor fails the moment "simple" stops sufficiently explaining the facts. A theory that's elegant but leaves evidence on the floor isn't simpler — it's just smaller. Cut assumptions, never evidence.

Cutting too much isn't rigor. It's laziness wearing a lab coat.

06 / The Calibration

The skill is knowing when to cut.

Anyone can pick the simple answer. The practitioner's edge is calibration — sensing when the simple answer is sufficient and when the facts genuinely demand a heavier theory.

The test, every time

Hold each explanation against the full set of facts. Strike any that leave a fact unexplained. Among the survivors, the lightest wins your first bet. Then go gather the one piece of evidence that would break it. If it survives that, you're calibrated.

07 / Closing — Take It to the Field

For the room you're walking into.

When a client, a student, or a team hands you a tangle of possible causes, they almost always hand you the elaborate one first — because elaborate feels like effort, and effort feels like care. Your job is to resist the performance and find the load-bearing wall.

Bench Notes · carry these
  • Count the assumptions, not the words. The longest theory often hides the most unproven claims. List them out loud.
  • Name the single sufficient cause first. Ask: what's the one thing that, if true, explains everything on the table?
  • Distrust the story that flatters someone. "They're brilliant but blocked by X" is elaborate comfort. Check it.
  • Cut assumptions, guard evidence. The instant simplifying drops a real fact, stop cutting.
  • Look first, don't convict. The lean theory earns your investigation — not your certainty.
08 / Off the Record
🪒 A thing worth knowing

William of Ockham never wrote the razor.

The principle bears his name, but the phrase "Occam's razor" was coined centuries after his death — and the famous Latin line entia non sunt multiplicanda appears nowhere in his surviving work. He argued the idea in scattered, clunkier forms; later scholars sharpened it, named it after him, and let history do the polishing.

Which is its own small joke. The cleanest version of an idea about cleanliness was itself an elaboration — assembled, attributed, and refined by people who came after. Even the razor needed editing.
