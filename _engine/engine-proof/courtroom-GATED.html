A team shipped six weeks late. Four theories explain why. Only one survives.

You are the judge. You will not be told the answer — you will rule on it.

Opening · Before the evidence

What does your gut say caused the delay?

Commit before you see the case. This is on the record.

Select your instinct to proceed.
Evidence · The four theories

Rank them by how much they ask you to believe.

Heaviest (most assumptions) at top → lightest at bottom. Use the arrows.

Move every theory at least once to return a verdict.
Verdict · The hidden weight

Each theory carried assumptions you couldn't see.

One theory needs only a single assumption and explains every fact. Tap it to deliver the cut.

Identify the survivor.
Ruling

Occam's Razor is not
"the simplest is true."

It is: start where the assumptions are fewest. Not because the simple answer is always right — but because it is where you should look first, and what you must rule out before reaching for the elaborate one.

You believed the heavy theory. Most people do. The razor is the discipline of noticing that — and cutting.

For reaching the end

Sea otters hold hands while they sleep.

Not for warmth. For anchorage. Adrift on the open water, an otter can wake to find the current has carried it far from everyone it knows. So before they sleep, they reach out and take the paw of another — sometimes a whole raft of them, linked, drifting together so none drifts alone.

Pups who haven't yet learned the habit are sometimes wrapped in giant kelp by their mothers — moored to the seabed like a small boat — so they stay put while she dives.

No mechanism. No lesson. Just something true and good, because you walked all the way here.
