The Barton Gap

LIVING INCOME · W: WEALTH · MELIORISM2.COM EDITORIAL

On May 21, 1881 — 145 years ago today — Clara Barton signed the charter of the American Red Cross. She was 59 years old. She had spent her thirties nursing soldiers in the Civil War, often without pay. She had spent her forties running the Office of Missing Soldiers, a U.S. government bureau she essentially invented and ran for free until Congress formalized it. She had spent her fifties lobbying for the Geneva Convention's adoption in the United States.

At every stage, her work was unambiguously valuable. At every stage, the rate attached to it was wrong. The moment the rate changed was not the moment her work changed. It was the moment the container changed.

This issue is about that gap — the distance between the value of your work and the price of your work — and what the research shows about why that gap persists even when the practitioner is excellent.

"I may sometimes be willing to teach for nothing, but if paid at all, I shall never do a man's work for less than a man's pay." — Clara Barton, 1854

She said that at 32. It took her another 27 years to close her own gap.
